Music Recommendation on the Million Song Dataset
Item-item collaborative filtering on Spark
Aryan, Sathvik


WHAT WE DID

We built a music recommender on the Million Song Dataset (the Echo Nest Taste
Profile, about 48 million play records). The idea is simple - given the songs a
person has listened to, recommend songs they haven't heard yet by finding songs
that tend to get played by the same people. The heart of the project, the
song-to-song similarity, is written from scratch using only Spark RDDs.

We developed and tested everything on a small sample locally, then ran the full
dataset on the Cal Poly YARN cluster (Spark 2.3.2). Our claim was that a
personalized recommender should clearly beat just handing everyone the most
popular songs, and it does, by a wide margin.


WHY IT NEEDS A CLUSTER

The input is around 3 GB (48,373,586 records, 1,019,318 users, 384,546 songs),
but the real reason a single machine struggles is the computation, not the file
size. To compare songs we generate, for each user, a pair for every two songs
they played. A user with n songs produces n-choose-2 pairs, so active users blow
up into huge numbers of pairs. On our full run this came out to roughly 387
million pairs and about 5.2 GB of shuffle. That explosion is why the work has to
be split across machines, and the same program would run on 10x the data just by
adding more machines.


HOW IT WORKS

The pipeline runs in a few stages, all in Spark RDDs:

- Ingest and re-index. Read the tab-separated triplets (user, song, play count)
  and map the long hash IDs to small integers, which are much cheaper to shuffle.

- Filter and transform. Drop songs and users with too little data to be reliable,
  and take a log of the play counts so heavy listeners don't dominate.

- Build user profiles. Group by user to get each user's list of (song, score),
  capping how many songs one user contributes to keep the pair explosion in check.

- Item-item cosine similarity (the from-scratch core). For each user we emit a
  record for every pair of songs they played, keyed by the song pair, with the
  product of the two scores as the value. A reduceByKey sums those products across
  all users, which gives the dot product (the top of the cosine formula) for each
  song pair. We compute each song's norm separately and divide to get the cosine
  similarity. Pairs are always ordered the same way so nothing is double counted,
  and we guard against divide-by-zero.

- Tame the explosion. We prune pairs that co-occur too rarely and keep only the
  top-K most similar songs per song, which bounds the model size.

- Recommend. For a user, look up the most-similar songs to everything they've
  played, add up the similarity-weighted scores, drop songs they already heard,
  and return the top N.

- Evaluate. For each user we hide half their listening history, build
  recommendations from the visible half, and measure how much of the hidden half
  we recover. We compare against a popularity baseline that just recommends the
  most-played songs to everyone.


RESULTS

Run on the full dataset, evaluated over 1,019,139 users with top-10
recommendations (k = 10):

  Metric      Our model    Popularity baseline    Lift
  ---------------------------------------------------------
  Precision   0.233        0.044                  5.3x
  Recall      0.137        0.028                  4.9x
  MAP         0.181        0.025                  7.1x
  F1          0.172        0.034                  5.0x

In plain terms - about 2.3 of every 10 songs we recommended were songs the user
actually went on to play, and those 10 picks covered roughly 14% of everything
they played. The popularity baseline lands 5 to 7 times lower on every metric, so
the model is clearly learning real co-listening structure and not just guessing.

For scale and cost - the full end-to-end evaluation took about 1 hour 37 minutes
on the cluster, mostly in the pair-generation and shuffle stages described above.


TAKEAWAY

The transferable lesson is how a "compare every pair of items" algorithm becomes
a practical distributed computation - emit contributions locally per user,
aggregate them globally with reduceByKey, and control the combinatorial blowup
with filtering and pruning. The music recommendations are the hook, the
map-emit-then-reduce pattern and the pair-explosion problem are the real lesson.
